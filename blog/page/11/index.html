Main Difference Between Intel and AT&T Syntax Assembly Language

Dec 11th, 2013 | Comments

  • AT&T immediate operands use a $ to denote them, whereas Intel immediate operands are undelimited. Thus, when referencing the decimal value 4 in AT&T syntax, you would use dollar 4, and in Intel syntax you would just use 4.
  • AT&T prefaces register names with a %, while Intel does not. Thus, referencing the EAX register in AT&T syntax, you would use %eax.
  • AT&T syntax uses the opposite order for source and destination operands. To move the decimal value 4 to the EAX register, AT&T syntax would be movl $4, %eax, whereas for Intel it would be mov eax, 4.
  • AT&T syntax uses a separate character at the end of mnemonics to reference the data size used in the operation, whereas in Intel syntax the size is declared as a separate operand. The AT&T instruction movl $test, %eax is equivalent to mov eax, dword ptr test in Intel syntax.
  • Long calls and jumps use a different syntax to define the segment and offset values. AT&T syn- tax uses ljmp $section, $offset, whereas Intel syntax uses jmp section:offset.

from: Professional Assembly Language, Richard Blum

How to Do Presentation

Dec 9th, 2013 | Comments

Just got some illuminating advice about doing presentation. They come from the teaching team of ME310 course of Stanford University. Share it here.

Will provide more after as I learn more.

  1. Virtually all photos are ambiguous on first sight by persons not previously involved with your project. THEREFORE, all photos are made more informative when annotated with accents, labels, call-outs, whatever. This also applies to sketches and CAD images.

  2. Avoid complex (multi-part) images. Focus on one-message per slide. This often translates to one graphic, table, diagram, or photo per slide. If it is worth showing, it is worth making it intelligible with a minimum of time and attention by the audience.

  3. SHOW ME, don’t tell me. Use images rather than words. Follow the ancient dictum to show, not tell.

  4. Walk the audience through the flow of the diagram, image, visualization if the image is a relatively complex (more than 3 elements).

  5. Prioritize, what are the 1-3 most important things you want the audience to remember. Craft your narrative around these KEY points.

  6. Simplify/Clarify, make each slide as clear, unambiguous and memorable as possible. Delete uninformative graphics, backgrounds, and logos. Make everything READABLE from anywhere in the audience given the presentation technology (that you have tested).

Non Greedy Regex

Nov 19th, 2013 | Comments

My RE isn’t matching/deleting what I want it to. (Or, “Greedy vs. stingy pattern matching”)

The two most common causes for this problem are:

  1. misusing the ‘.’ metacharacter
  2. misusing the ‘*’ metacharacter.

The RE ‘.*’ is designed to be “greedy” (i.e., matching as many characters as possible). However, sometimes users need an expression which is “stingy,” matching the shortest possible string.
