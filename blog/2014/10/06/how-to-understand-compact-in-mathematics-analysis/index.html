finite

Though the concept of finite and infinite predates college, it seems that I do not pay enough attention to them till now. But note that even without mentioning this concept, the meaning of finite in the definition of compact is still straightforward.

Definition For any positive integer n, let $J_{n}$ be the set whose elements are the integers $1, 2, \ldots, n$; let $J$ be the set consisting of all positive integers. For any set $A$, we say:

  1. $A$ is finite if $A ~ J_{n}$ for some $n$ (the empty set is also considered to be finite).
  2. $A$ is infinite if $A$ is not finite.
  3. $A$ is countable if $A ~ J$.
  4. $A$ is uncountable if $A$ is neither finite nor countable.
  5. $A$ is at most countable if $A$ is finite or countable.

The above definition uses the notion of equivalence.

Equivalence

Definition If there exists a $1-1$ mapping of $A$ onto $B$, we say that $A$ and $B$ can be put in $1-1$ correspondence, or that $A$ and $B$ have the same cardinal number, or briefly, that $A$ and $B$ are equivalent, and we write $A \sim B$.

compact explained

Now, let’s try to understand the idea of compact. First we see one set that is not compact. Then we try to see the reason why compact is put forward. The main intuition is, I think, which is not cited from anywhere else, so it may be wrong, that the introduce of compact bridges the gap between the set’s global properties and local properties, infinite properties and finite properties.

Example

The open interval $I = (0, 1)$ is not compact.

To see why, let . Each is an open cover of $I$ and as $n \rightarrow \infty$, could cover $I$. However, if we only take finite number of subcover of , we cannot cover $I$.

However, if we let $I$ to be a closed interval, say $I = [0, 1]$, it is compact. A general theorem about this is described as every k-cell(try Google what k-cell is if you have no idea what it is) is compact. Proof here will be omitted(Proof can be found on Theorem 2.40 of Ref 1). The intuition behind the proof is that if an open cover $G$ could be found, then if any finite subcovers of $G$ cannot cover the k-cell, you can always add more but finite number of subcovers to cover all of $G$.

So why only a difference of open and closed makes such a difference?

In my view, the key difference is the limit point. If we do not makes arbitrary statement about the limit point, which is the boundary in this case, abnormal phenomenon could happen when approaching the limit point. As in this case, when point, denoted $x$, is approaching zero, if some function $f$ defined on the interval and $f$ has $x$ in the denominator, $f$ may be boundless.

Thus, compact models the process that a point is approaching its limit step by step(open ball by open ball) without making arbitrary statement about the boundary. Consequently, compact has a number of connection with continuity and convergence.

In the following, we would see more examples that compact is in action to have a feeling about its effect.

One More Example

Theorem Compact subsets of metric spaces are closed.

Proof The proof also starts from the intuition that if the subset is open, we cannot approach the boundary of the subset, since they are not part of the set, in a finite step.

Before we prove the theorem, a common misunderstanding of the compact should be pointed out. It is also straightforward to think that if one set $G$ is compact, it could be covered by a finite subcover, therefore, any subsets of $G$ could also be covered by such subcover. Consequently, any subsets of $G$ is also compact.

The misunderstanding under this reasoning is that compact is defined by all open covers, which means it has to rule out all possible open covers. Note that the open covers of $G$ cannot cover all open covers of its subsets. For example, a open cover could cover one subset $G_i$ of $G$, which indeed is an open cover of $G_i$, but this open cover is not one of $G$.

Now, the proof is begun(Ref 2).

One More Example…

Theorem Closed subsets of compact sets are compact.

Proof Ref 1. The intuition behind this proof is that for each finite open cover of $K$, there exists a finite subcover that could cover $F$, which means $F$ is compact.

Suppose $F \subset K \subset X$, $F$ is closed (relative to $X$), and $K$ is compact. Let ${V_{\alpha}}$ be an open cover of $F$. If we add one more open set $F^{c}$, then ${V_{\alpha}} \cup F^{c}$ becomes the open cover of $K$. Since $K$ is compact, there exists finite subcovers that would cover $K$. By excluding $F^{c}$, those subcovers are also a finite cover of $F$, which means $F$ is compact.
